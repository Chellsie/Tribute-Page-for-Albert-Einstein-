A German-born theoretical physicist

Albert Einstein

Early Life

Albert Einstein, born on March 14, 1879, in Ulm, Germany, was a theoretical physicist who developed the theory of relativity, one of the two pillars of modern physics.


Einstein was born to a secular Jewish family. His father, Hermann Einstein, was an engineer, and his mother, Pauline Koch, was a pianist. He had one younger sister, Maja. As a child, Einstein was quiet and slow to speak but showed an early fascination with the world, particularly after receiving a compass from his father at age five. He also developed a love for mathematics, mastering calculus by age fourteen after discovering a geometry book.


He attended Catholic elementary school and later the Luitpold Gymnasium in Munich, where he disliked the rigid teaching methods. Excelling in math and science, he struggled with other subjects and developed a distrust of authority. At 15, he dropped out of school and joined his family in Italy, who had moved there due to his father's business difficulties.


At 16, Einstein attempted the entrance exam for the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology (ETH) in Zurich but failed some sections despite excelling in math and physics. After a year at a more independent-thinking Swiss secondary school, he successfully gained admission to ETH in 1896 to study physics and mathematics. Despite disliking formal lectures, he graduated in 1900. After struggling to find a teaching job, he became a patent examiner in Bern in 1902, a position that allowed him to develop his revolutionary theories.


Key Achievements and Inventions

Theory of Relativity: Einstein developed both the special and general theories of relativity. These theories revolutionized our understanding of space, time, gravity, and the universe.
Photoelectric Effect: His explanation of the photoelectric effect, which describes how light can cause electrons to be emitted from a material, earned him the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921.
Mass-Energy Equivalence (E=mcÂ²): This famous equation demonstrates the relationship between mass and energy, showing that mass can be onverted into a large amount of energy and vice versa.
Brownian Motion: Einstein's work on Brownian motion, the erratic movement of particles suspended in a fluid, provided strong evidence for the existence of atoms and molecules.
Quantum Theory of Light: Einstein contributed to the development of quantum theory by proposing that light can behave as both a wave and a particle (wave-particle duality), and that light consists of packets of energy called photons.
Bose-Einstein Condensate: Einstein's work on the behavior of particles at extremely low temperatures laid the foundation for the concept of the Bose-Einstein condensate.
Refrigerator Design: Along with Leo Szilard, Einstein also invented a refrigerator that did not require electricity.

Awards and Recognition:

Nobel Prize in Physics (1921): Awarded for his explanation of the photoelectric effect.
Max Planck Medal (1929): A prestigious award from the German Physical Society.
Copley Medal (1925): The highest award of the Royal Society, recognizing his profound impact on the scientific community.
Numerous Honorary Doctorates: Einstein received ten honorary doctorates from various institutions between 1909 and 1935.
Time Person of the Century (1999):Recognized for his profound influence on the 20th century.
